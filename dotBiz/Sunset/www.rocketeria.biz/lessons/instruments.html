GUITAR

  • In general, we don't advise beginning guitar lessons before age 8 due to the level of hand strength and dexterity required. That being said, inspiration is a powerful motivator!
  • It doesn't really matter what kind of guitar you learn on, as long as it's in good working order. Though you may be reluctant to invest in something you're not sure about (Weekly students get a 10% discount), no amount of tenacity can overcome a defective instrument. Bring in what you've got—we'll check to see if it'll do the job.
  • Many "old school" guitar teachers say you must start on a nylon string (Classical) guitar. Yes, the softer strings are easier on your fingers, but the fretboard is wider and the strings are often further from the fretboard, requiring more stretch and strength.
  • Sometimes a sized guitar is appropriate, usually for kids 9 or 10 and younger. Let us help you decide if you need that 3/4 size guitar, or if the full size is OK.

 

BASS

  • A bass guitar is similar to the regular ole' 6-string guitar described above, in that the lowest 4 strings are the same on both instruments. 
  • We teach electric, acoustic, and upright (aka double bass).

 

PIANO

  • We teach on two acoustic pianos, one digital piano, a 64 and an 88-key electronic piano with weighted keys. These are distributed throughout our lessons studios. 
  • For practice at home, at least for beginners, an inexpensive keyboard is sufficient. Beyond that stage, we recommend one of the options we teach on. For many, the digital piano or electronic keyboard is the most affordable
    and practical.
  • Our acoustic pianos are regularly maintained by Clyde Allen—local, super trustworthy and professional. And a really nice man.

 

DRUMS

  • We primarily teach on real drums. We have one electronic kit, mostly used for full band rehearsals in our performance space, "Room One", to control volume when other lessons are taking place. Electronic drums have gotten really good, and are an excellent solution for many drummers who need to practice on a full kit without offending their own or neighbors' households.
  • This is a great starter instrument! Little kids, 5 or 6, love the freedom of playing drums, and as a foundation, you can't get more crucial than rhythm.
  • Percussion as taught in school band is less likely to involve full drum set playing, more snare drum, bells (xylophone), cymbals, triangle and other hand-held percussion. Yes, we teach these too!

 

VOICE

  • We teach contemporary vocal technique and style as well as microphone technique, stage presence and performance.
  • Emphasis on ear training, harmonizing, improvisation.
  • Voice students should warm up before class. YouTube is sick with vocal warmup tutorials, or just sing along to your favorite songs in the car on the way to class.
  • We do not teach classical or musical theatre voice.
     

SAXOPHONE

  • We teach on Alto Saxophone, a good beginner woodwind instrument with similar fingerings to the Clarinet.
  • The student will learn proper form and posture, how to read music, some beginner theory, and Jazz Standards.
  • The saxophone is a great instrument for students interested in rock, blues, and Jazz. Many middle schools in the area have Jazz Bands and almost every high school has one.
  • Saxophones are less common in a high school orchestra setting where the clarinet is the more prominently featured woodwind.
  • The saxophone is a relatively heavy instrument that uses a neck strap for support. For younger students (below age 9) it is recommended that they try holding one with a neck strap while sitting down to make sure they will be comfortable during lessons, in class, and in performance situations.

 

CLARINET

  • We teach on Bb Clarinet, Beginner only

  • The clarinet is a fundamental instrument in any school band or orchestra. It is a woodwind, like the saxophone, but slightly smaller and easier to carry than an alto saxophone.

  • The clarinet is featured heavily in school bands and is one of the most common instruments to begin a musical career on.

  • The clarinet does require the student to be able to cover entire holes with their fingertips as opposed to simply pressing a key down (the saxophone has closed keys only). It is recommended to have the prospective student try holding one in their hands to see if they can support the instrument with both hands and reach the necessary keys.

 

MANDOLIN

  • Mandolins have two strings for every pitch compared to most stringed instruments, which only have one.

  • They have high tension strings and can be tough to finger, so it's not recommended as a starting instrument for younger students.

  • They are tuned the same as violins.

  • Fun Fact: R.E.M.'s Losing My Religion was the first number one song with mandolin on it.

 

TENOR BANJO

  • A tenor banjo is not the same as a bluegrass banjo, which has five strings. It is a short necked banjo that has 4-strings.  

  • The tenor banjo was originally made for viola players to double on, that is why it is traditionally tuned the same as a viola.  

  • The “Irish” tuning is the same as violin, only an octave lower. This makes it easy to play fiddle melodies in Irish music.

 

5-STRING BANJO

  • A 5-string banjo is the instrument most commonly used when playing in a bluegrass style, clawhammer or finger-picking.

UKULELE

  • Our teachers will take you through the steps of learning to read music for the ukulele, and show you how to play your favorite pop and traditional folk songs.

  • Learn the basics of chords and strumming. 

  • The best additional instrument for guitar students!

  • A great instrument for younger players interested in guitars or string instruments.
